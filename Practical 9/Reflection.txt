Practical 9 reflection

This practical was challenging but useful because the same biological idea had to be represented in several different computational ways: a simple well-mixed SIR model, a vaccination model, and a spatial grid model. This helped me understand that a biological model is not only about writing code, but also about translating real-world assumptions into variables, probabilities, loops, and plots.

In the simple SIR model, I learned how beta and gamma control the outbreak. Beta affects how easily susceptible people become infected, while gamma controls how quickly infected people recover. One important point was that the infection probability is not just beta. It also depends on the proportion of infected people in the population, so I used beta multiplied by I / N. I also learned why the order of updates matters: infection and recovery events should be calculated first, and then the values of S, I, and R should be updated and recorded.

I was initially surprised that the model did not always produce a large outbreak. Sometimes the first infected person recovered before infecting many other people, so the infected curve stayed almost flat. After running the script several times, I realised that this was not necessarily a mistake. It showed that the model was stochastic rather than deterministic. The same parameters can still produce slightly different results because infection and recovery are chosen randomly at each time point.

The vaccination model helped me understand herd immunity more clearly. By adding a vaccinated group, I could see that reducing the susceptible population lowers the peak number of infected people. As the vaccination percentage increased from 0% to 100%, the infected curves became smaller and flatter. For these parameter values, the approximate herd-immunity threshold seemed to be around 80% vaccination, although this value was not exact because the simulation was stochastic and changed between runs.

The spatial SIR model was the hardest part of the practical. In the well-mixed model, any susceptible person can effectively meet any infected person. In the spatial model, however, infection can only spread from an infected cell to its eight neighbouring cells. This made the model more realistic in one way, but also more difficult to code. I had to think carefully about how to find infected cells, how to check neighbouring cells, and how to handle edge and corner cells that do not have eight neighbours.

The most useful coding idea I learned from the spatial model was to use a copied version of the population grid for updates. This helped avoid changing the grid too early during the loop. If I updated the original grid immediately, newly infected cells might infect others in the same time step, which would make the spread faster than intended. Using a separate next_population grid made the update process clearer and more controlled.

I enjoyed the plotting part because the graphs made the model easier to understand. In the simple SIR plot, I could see susceptible people decreasing, infected people rising and then falling, and recovered people increasing. In the vaccination plot, I could compare different vaccination percentages directly. In the spatial plots, I could see the disease spreading locally through the grid, with recovered or resistant cells appearing behind the infection front.

One difficulty I had was understanding why the output looked different each time I ran the same script. At first, I expected the same parameters to give the same figure every time. Later, I understood that this variation is exactly what should happen in a probabilistic model. Another practical difficulty was making sure that plots were saved in the correct folder and that the axes, titles, and legends were clearly labelled.

The most important thing I learned from this practical is that stochastic models can represent uncertainty in biological systems. They do not give one fixed answer, but they can show possible patterns and help us understand how parameters such as infection probability, recovery probability, vaccination rate, and spatial structure affect disease spread. If I improved this work further, I would run each model many times and calculate average curves, because a single stochastic run may not represent the overall behaviour of the system.
